### Competency: Patient Care & Procedural Skills (ACGME)

**Competency description:**
Provides compassionate, appropriate, and effective care; gathers accurate data,
forms differentials and management plans, and performs procedures safely.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Workplace-based direct-observation rating scale.
  - Tool name: Mini-Clinical Evaluation Exercise (mini-CEX).
  - Context/setting: Real clinical encounters (clinic, ward, ED).
  - Target learner group: Residents, clinical clerks.
  - Source publication(s): Citation 1: [R1] Norcini et al., 2003.
- Tool type: Procedural WBA checklist + global rating.
  - Tool name: Direct Observation of Procedural Skills (DOPS).
  - Context/setting: Bedside/procedural (endoscopy, surgery, ophthalmology, etc.).
  - Target learner group: Postgraduate trainees.
  - Source publication(s): [R2] Norcini & Burch (AMEE Guide 31), 2007; [R3] DOPS validity review, 2023; [R4] DOPS systematic review, 2018.
- Tool type: Entrustment-supervision scale (procedural).
  - Tool name: Ottawa Surgical Competency Operating Room Evaluation (O-SCORE).
  - Context/setting: Operating room; adaptable to other procedures.
  - Target learner group: Surgical/procedural residents.
  - Source publication(s): [R5] Gofton et al., 2012.
- Tool type: Objective Structured Clinical Examination (OSCE) station scoring.
  - Source publication(s): [R6] Regehr et al., 1998; [R7] Hodges et al., 1999.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Primarily formative feedback; mini-CEX/DOPS aggregate toward summative judgments.
- Competency elements operationalized: History, physical exam, clinical judgment, procedural technique, indications/consent, adaptation to new data.
- Scoring approach: Anchored rating scales (mini-CEX 9-point), procedural checklists + global ratings (DOPS), entrustment/supervision anchors (O-SCORE: "how much intervention did the learner need?").
- Development process: mini-CEX psychometric study across 21 IM programs; DOPS via generalisability studies; O-SCORE via expert development + validity studies.
- Training requirements: Rater training and multiple encounters/assessors are needed for reliable scores.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: mini-CEX generates reliable ratings and correlates with other performance assessments [R1]. DOPS reliability reaches G>=0.70 with ~6 observations from >=3 assessors [R3][R4]. O-SCORE discriminates skill/training level and improves reliability vs norm-referenced tools [R5].
- Strengths highlighted: High fidelity, on-site, promotes feedback and independence; broad case coverage.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Single-encounter/single-rater design makes inter-rater reliability hard to establish; case specificity; need many observations for stable scores [R1][R3][R4].
- Practical limitations: Time to observe, record, and give feedback; scheduling; resource constraints [R4].
- Conceptual limitations: Formative vs summative purpose confusion undermines validity; O-SCORE is a RETROSPECTIVE supervision scale and is insufficient alone for prospective/summative entrustment [R3][R5b][R5c].
- Contextual limitations: Strong validity shown only for specific DOPS (endoscopy, ENT); generalizability uncertain [R3].
- Author-reported concerns: Trainees may "cherry-pick" easy cases or select lenient assessors ("gaming") [R3]; assessor subjectivity and bias persist [R4].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Video-based observation (VOPS) to blind raters, reduce bias, and allow inter-rater reliability [R3].
- Multiple assessors per procedure; clearer separation of formative/summative use.
- Better entrustment frameworks that combine retrospective WBA data with committee judgment [R5b].

### Competency: Medical Knowledge (ACGME)

**Competency description:**
Demonstrates knowledge of established and evolving biomedical, clinical, and
cognate sciences and applies it to patient care.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Multiple-choice knowledge test / annual In-Training Examination (ITE).
  - Context/setting: Written, program- or board-administered.
  - Target learner group: Residents/fellows.
- Tool type: Clinical-reasoning-under-uncertainty test.
  - Tool name: Script Concordance Test (SCT).
  - Context/setting: Written vignettes with expert reference panel scoring.
  - Target learner group: Students, residents.
  - Source publication(s): [R8] Lubarsky et al., 2011 (validity review).

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Summative knowledge measurement (MCQ/ITE); assessment of clinical reasoning under uncertainty (SCT).
- Competency elements operationalized: Factual/applied knowledge (MCQ); interpretation of ambiguous data against expert panels (SCT).
- Scoring approach: Keyed MCQ; SCT aggregate scoring against expert panel response distribution.
- Development process: SCT requires a reference panel (>=~15 experts recommended); items nested in cases.
- Training requirements: MCQ minimal; SCT requires panel recruitment and item-writing expertise.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: MCQs are a widely accepted, reliable/valid measure of knowledge. SCT shows construct validity (scores rise with experience) and evidence for content, internal structure, and relations to other variables [R8].
- Strengths highlighted: Efficient, scalable, objective (MCQ); SCT captures reasoning MCQs miss.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: SCT aggregate scoring is criticized for construct-irrelevant variance (midpoint-response gaming); reliability depends on panel size; evidence for response process and consequences is weak [R8].
- Practical limitations: SCT item construction and expert-panel recruitment are difficult; MCQs poorly capture reasoning/performance.
- Conceptual limitations: Knowledge tests do not measure application in practice ("knows"/"knows how" vs "shows"/"does").
- Contextual limitations: SCT validity argument limited by a small literature base and researcher bias [R8].
- Author-reported concerns: Little known about SCT long-term educational impact; no outcomes data to defend high-stakes use [R8].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Optimize/settle SCT scoring methods; strengthen consequence validity.
- Blueprint knowledge tests to competencies; triangulate with performance-based tools.

### Competency: Practice-Based Learning & Improvement (PBLI) (ACGME)

**Competency description:**
Investigates and evaluates one's own care, appraises/assimilates evidence, and
continuously improves via reflection and quality improvement (QI).

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: PBLI-specific questionnaire/coding rubric.
  - Context/setting: Residency; guides curriculum development.
  - Source publication(s): [R9] PBLI assessment tool, J Grad Med Educ, 2011.
- Tool type: Evidence-based medicine (EBM) competence tests (see also Scholar).
  - Tool name(s): Fresno test; Berlin questionnaire; ACE tool.
  - Source publication(s): [R17] Ramos et al., 2003 (Fresno); [R18] Fritsche et al., 2002 (Berlin); [R19] Ilic, 2009 (review); [R20] ACE tool, 2014.
- Tool type: Portfolios, medical-record audit, QI-project evaluation, performance dashboards, global ratings.
  - Source publication(s): [R10] Kessler et al. (ACEM consensus), 2012; [R11] PBLI dashboard/reflective-practice analysis, 2021.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Formative + programmatic; supports CCC judgments.
- Competency elements operationalized: PBLI-1 evidence-based/informed practice (well-defined, EBM-testable) vs PBLI-2 reflective practice/commitment to growth (novel, hard to measure) [R11].
- Scoring approach: Coded open responses, checklist/rubric items, dashboard metrics, project rubrics.
- Development process: Expert input, pilot testing; single-institution samples common.
- Training requirements: Faculty development strongly emphasized.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: Preliminary reliability/validity for the PBLI tool [R9]; dashboards improve feedback satisfaction [R11]; Fresno provides objective EBM measurement across all four EBM steps [R17].
- Strengths highlighted: Aligns assessment with reflection and EBM; data transparency.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Lack of validated, reliable tools; poor inter-rater reliability; no agreed "criterion standard"; no established competency cut-points [R9][R10].
- Practical limitations: Manual audits/portfolio review are time-intensive; dashboard metrics may not be attributable to or controllable by the individual trainee [R11].
- Conceptual limitations: PBLI-2 (reflective practice) is novel and many faculty feel unable to assess it; global ratings miss specific gaps [R11].
- Contextual limitations: Tools often single-program, single-specialty; generalizability unproven [R9][R10].
- Author-reported concerns: Educators understand PBLI concepts but "struggle with how to assess" it; residents may see logs/reflection as busywork [R11].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Cross-specialty dissemination of validated tools; triangulation of multiple data sources.
- Faculty development for reflective-practice assessment; better EHR-linked dashboards.

### Competency: Interpersonal & Communication Skills (ACGME)

**Competency description:**
Effective information exchange and relationship-building with patients, families,
and teams, including documentation and handoffs.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Communication task checklist.
  - Tool name: SEGUE Framework (Set stage, Elicit info, Give info, Understand perspective, End encounter).
  - Context/setting: Standardized-patient encounters, real practice, undergraduate/graduate.
  - Source publication(s): [R12] Makoul, 2001 (SEGUE).
- Tool type: Consensus-based communication checklist.
  - Tool name: Kalamazoo Essential Elements Communication Checklist (KEECC / KEECC-A / Gap-Kalamazoo).
  - Source publication(s): [R13] Makoul, 2001 (Kalamazoo consensus); [R14] Joyce et al., 2010 (KEECC-A).

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Teaching, formative, and summative communication assessment.
- Competency elements operationalized: Relationship-building, information gathering/giving, perspective-taking, shared decision-making, closure.
- Scoring approach: Task checklists (SEGUE); rating anchors mapped to 7 essential elements (Kalamazoo).
- Development process: SEGUE research-based; Kalamazoo via expert consensus statement.
- Training requirements: SP/rater training; immediate post-encounter completion improves reliability.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: SEGUE has high inter-/intra-rater reliability and concurrent/construct validity; most widely used tool in North America [R12]. KEECC-A is psychometrically sound and user-friendly across specialties [R14].
- Strengths highlighted: High acceptability, links to expert consensus, multi-rater usability.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: SEGUE lacks predictive-validity studies [R12]; original KEECC scales had no published psychometrics; inter-rater reliability of Kalamazoo tools is only modest [R13].
- Practical limitations: Checklists can reward rote task completion over genuine communication quality.
- Conceptual limitations: Convergent/criterion validity for Kalamazoo tools not firmly established [R13].
- Contextual limitations: Much validity evidence derives from SP/simulated settings; transfer to messy real encounters uncertain.
- Author-reported concerns: "Studies of predictive validity are needed" [R12]; continuing validation required [R13].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Predictive-validity and real-workplace studies; adaptation for family/multiparty encounters.

### Competency: Professionalism (ACGME)

**Competency description:**
Commitment to carrying out professional responsibilities and adherence to ethical
principles (integrity, accountability, respect, autonomy).

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Professionalism WBA rating form.
  - Tool name: Professionalism Mini-Evaluation Exercise (P-MEX).
  - Context/setting: Clinical observation; validated across Canada, Japan, Iran, Finland, Switzerland.
  - Target learner group: Students, residents.
  - Source publication(s): [R15] Cruess et al., 2006 (preliminary); [R16] P-MEX 8-year validity, 2021.
- Tool type: Multisource (360-degree) feedback.
  - Source publication(s): [R2] Norcini & Burch, 2007.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Formative feedback + identification of "problem" behaviors; aggregate summative.
- Competency elements operationalized: Doctor-patient relationship, reflective skills, time management, interprofessional skills [R15].
- Scoring approach: Behaviorally anchored ratings across ~24 items; some items "not applicable" by setting.
- Development process: Factor analysis (4 factors); generalisability/decision studies; cross-cultural adaptations.
- Training requirements: Multiple observations required (10-12 forms for G~0.80; up to 14 occasions reported) [R15][R16].

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: Content and construct validity; actionable subscores; cross-cultural response-process evidence [R15][R16].
- Strengths highlighted: Promotes self-reflection and awareness; feasible for routine use.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Rater variation and case specificity; low generalizability when repeated over a year (ceiling — high scores from the outset with little variation) [R16].
- Practical limitations: Major limitation is TIME — time to observe, record, and give feedback [R15].
- Conceptual limitations: Threats of construct underrepresentation and construct-irrelevant variance; some items not applicable to trainee scope [R16].
- Contextual limitations: Needs local cross-cultural adaptation; many studies simulated/one-time-point [R16].
- Author-reported concerns: Professionalism is context-dependent and behavior-based; single ratings insufficient.

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Longitudinal, multilevel modeling; combine P-MEX with MSF and critical-incident reporting.

### Competency: Systems-Based Practice (SBP) (ACGME)

**Competency description:**
Awareness of and responsiveness to the larger system of care; coordinates care,
practices cost-consciously, and advocates within systems for patients.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Multisource / 360-degree feedback; QI-project performance evaluation; patient-safety & quality-of-care performance data; medical-record audit; OSCE/simulation (limited).
  - Source publication(s): [R21] "Orphan competency" review, 2021; [R22] Graham et al. (SBP taxonomy), 2009; [R23] ACGME Assessment Guidebook (Holmboe & Iobst), 2020; [R24] CEMRD consensus, 2011.
- NOTE: No single validated SBP-specific instrument exists; assessment relies on a coordinated combination of methods.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Programmatic assessment via CCC using multiple data sources.
- Competency elements operationalized: Patient safety/QI, care coordination, cost/resource stewardship, system navigation.
- Scoring approach: MSF ratings, QI-project rubrics, performance dashboards, milestone mapping.
- Development process: Taxonomy/consensus development; performance-data linkage.
- Training requirements: Faculty understanding of SBP is itself a barrier; QI/patient-safety literacy needed.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: SBP milestone judgments feasible when performance data are viewed through a "contribution" lens rather than strict attribution [R23].
- Strengths highlighted: Performance data create transparency; MSF captures interprofessional system behavior.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: "Limited validity evidence"; unclear whether methods predict performance; validated tools for physician behavior within systems have NOT been developed [R21].
- Practical limitations: Attribution of clinical/system outcomes to an individual trainee is difficult; metrics may be outside trainee control [R21][R23].
- Conceptual limitations: SBP is broad and interlinked with other competencies and hard to separate/measure directly [R24].
- Contextual limitations: Teaching and assessment inconsistent/variable across programs; the "orphan" competency [R21].
- Author-reported concerns: Many residents graduate with a persistent SBP knowledge/skill gap; passive learning and indirect observation without objective criteria predominate [R21].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Feasible, generalizable, shareable SBP curricula + validated assessments; experiential, team-based assessment; specialty-neutral tools.

### Competency: Medical Expert (CanMEDS)

**Competency description:**
Integrates all CanMEDS Roles, applying medical knowledge, clinical skills, and
professional values to deliver patient-centered care. (Central, integrating role.)

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Direct-observation WBA and entrustment scales (see ACGME Patient Care & Medical Knowledge).
  - Tool names: mini-CEX [R1]; DOPS [R2][R3][R4]; O-SCORE / Ottawa Clinic Assessment Tool (OCAT) entrustment scales [R5]; EPAs [R25]; Script Concordance Test [R8].
  - CROSS-REFERENCE: See "Patient Care & Procedural Skills" and "Medical Knowledge".

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Formative WBA aggregated into entrustment decisions via EPAs.
- Competency elements operationalized: Integrated clinical decision-making, assessment/intervention appropriate to competence level.
- Scoring approach: Entrustment-supervision anchors; anchored rating scales.
- Development process: EPA/entrustment-scale development (Chen scale vs Ottawa scale) [R25][R26].
- Training requirements: Rater training; multiple EPA observations across time.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: Entrustment scales track supervision needs and rise with training level; learner variance comparable across Chen/Ottawa scales [R26].
- Strengths highlighted: Bridges abstract competency frameworks to daily practice via EPAs [R25].

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Rater variability and residual variance limit reliability of single WBAs; modest G-coefficients (~0.65) even with trained raters [R26]; WBAs alone insufficient for summative entrustment [R27].
- Practical limitations: Many observations needed; scale anchors vary across institutions, hindering comparison [R27].
- Conceptual limitations: Retrospective supervision ratings != prospective entrustment; individual WBAs do not capture the full entrustment construct [R5b].
- Contextual limitations: Evidence heavily from surgical/procedural and UME pilot settings.
- Author-reported concerns: Faculty-development effects on reliability are mixed and only theoretically supported [R27].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Robust programmatic-assessment models; standardized entrustment anchors; committee-based synthesis.

### Competency: Collaborator (CanMEDS)

**Competency description:**
Works effectively with physicians and other colleagues in interprofessional teams,
including safe handovers and shared/team-based care.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Interprofessional competency rubric.
  - Tool name: Interprofessional Collaborator Assessment Rubric (ICAR) — 31 items, 6 domains.
  - Context/setting: Multi-source feedback on hospital rotations; multi-profession.
  - Target learner group: Health-profession students, postgraduate residents.
  - Source publication(s): [R28] Curran et al., 2011 (development/validation); [R29] ICAR reliability in MSF, 2014.
- Tool type: Multisource (360-degree) feedback.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Formative feedback and summative achievement on collaboration.
- Competency elements operationalized: Communication, collaboration, roles/responsibilities, patient/family-centered care, conflict management/resolution, team functioning.
- Scoring approach: 5-point rubric across 31 statements (max 124).
- Development process: Typological analysis of frameworks -> 2-round Delphi (21 experts) -> 12 focus groups; bilingual EN/FR.
- Training requirements: Multiple raters across professions (physicians, nurses, allied health); trainees blinded to timing; >=6 assessments from >=2 rater groups.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: High content validity; discriminative validity across performance levels (minimal->mastery, p<.001); high internal consistency (alpha .88-.96) [R28][R29].
- Strengths highlighted: Cross-profession applicability; formative + summative use.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Conflict-resolution/management items had high missing data — hard to observe in well-functioning teams; interrater variability [R29].
- Practical limitations: Requires prolonged observation and many raters; patient participation hard to obtain [R29].
- Conceptual limitations: Original development study did not administer the tool to a sample (content-validity focus only) [R28].
- Contextual limitations: Reliability study single-institution, small physician/allied samples, uneven gender distribution; residents chose their own assessors [R29].
- Author-reported concerns: Prolonged observation periods likely needed for adequate Collaborator assessment [R29].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Multi-site validity/reliability; reducing interrater variability via rater training; methods to assess conflict management authentically.

### Competency: Leader (formerly Manager) (CanMEDS)

**Competency description:**
Engages in shaping/delivering healthcare via administration, resource stewardship,
personal leadership, and contribution to system improvement.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: Leadership multisource feedback tool.
  - Tool name: LEADER (Learning by Evaluation from All-inclusive 360-Degree Engagement of Residents).
  - Context/setting: Junior residents; 360 MSF.
  - Source publication(s): [R31] LEADER tool, BMJ Leader, 2021.
- Reference frameworks: LEADS in a Caring Environment; CanMEDS Assessment Tools Handbook [R33].
- STATUS: LARGELY UNDER-DEVELOPED / INSUFFICIENT PUBLISHED EVIDENCE for validated, reproducible Leader assessment.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Formative measurement of observable leadership behaviors in juniors.
- Competency elements operationalized: A SUBSET of the Leader construct only.
- Scoring approach: 360 MSF ratings.
- Development process: Tool development with MSF raters; expert-consensus based.
- Training requirements: Multiple assessors.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: LEADER can capture presence of some leadership competencies in junior residents [R31].
- Strengths highlighted: Provides 360 feedback where little else exists.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Generalisability study not feasible (uncontrolled assessor-occasion combinations); no divergent/convergent validity established; no consequential-validity data [R31].
- Practical limitations: Purposive/convenience sampling risk; degree of competence (vs mere presence) not measured [R31].
- Conceptual limitations: LEADER explicitly does NOT assess many Leader concepts (systems complexity, budgeting/financing, leading change, personnel management, supervising others) — better suited to senior residents/practitioners [R31].
- Contextual limitations: Leadership is abstract, context-dependent, and rarely directly observable; tools lack empirical leadership-specific validation [R31][R33].
- Author-reported concerns: Delphi study found NO Leader key competency deemed consistently assessable across settings/phases, and 3 of 4 Leader competencies rated not feasible for workplace assessment [R30]; program directors report "still searching for assessment tools" [R33].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Validated tools for senior-level leadership constructs; consequential-validity studies; patient/stakeholder perspectives.

### Competency: Scholar (CanMEDS)

**Competency description:**
Demonstrates lifelong learning, teaching, evidence appraisal/application (EBM), and
contribution to scholarship.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool type: EBM/EBP competence tests.
  - Tool names: Fresno test; Berlin questionnaire; ACE tool.
  - Source publication(s): [R17] Ramos et al., 2003 (Fresno); [R18] Fritsche et al., 2002 (Berlin); [R19] Ilic, 2009 (review); [R20] ACE tool, 2014.
- Tool type: Teaching-effectiveness assessment (learner evaluations / structured observation) — limited validated options.
- STATUS: EBM sub-competency has validated tools; teaching/scholarship sub-competencies are UNDER-DEVELOPED.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose of the tool: Summative/formative EBM competence measurement.
- Competency elements operationalized: Fresno — all 4 EBM steps (ask, acquire, appraise, apply); Berlin — appraisal/statistics (Step 3 only); ACE — appraisal-focused single scenario.
- Scoring approach: Fresno = open-ended + grading rubric; Berlin/ACE = MCQ.
- Development process: Fresno validated with residents; Berlin with course participants; ACE with medical trainees.
- Training requirements: Fresno requires expert graders (~60 min to complete + grading time); Berlin ~15-20 min.

**Evidence of effectiveness:**
- Reported outcomes: Fresno — good construct validity and inter-rater reliability; only standardized measure across all EBM steps [R17][R19]. Berlin — reliably distinguishes expertise [R18].
- Strengths highlighted: Objective, performance-based EBM measurement.

**Reported limitations and lackings:**
- Psychometric limitations: Fresno validation used proficiency extremes (middle under-represented); ceiling effects; single vignette set (recall bias); ACE items on bias performed poorly [R17][R20].
- Practical limitations: Fresno resource-intensive to grade; Berlin narrow (only appraisal/Step 3) [R19].
- Conceptual limitations: Neither Fresno nor Berlin covers all 5 EBM steps; both validated only in medicine; the two correlate poorly and are not interchangeable [R19][R20]. Relies on expert opinion as standard rather than patient outcomes [R17].
- Contextual limitations: Limited to medical populations; teaching/scholarship assessment lacks validated tools.
- Author-reported concerns: Practising physicians care about patient outcomes, which no EBM test measures [R17]; consensus not reached on consistent workplace assessment for some Scholar competencies [R30].

**Gaps and future directions (from the literature or inferred):**
- Multi-scenario, discipline-general EBM tools covering all 5 steps; OSCE-based EBM assessment [R19]; validated teaching-effectiveness instruments.

### Competency: Professional (CanMEDS)

**Competency description:**
Commitment to patients, society, and the profession through ethical practice,
high standards, accountability, and physician well-being.

**Assessment tools identified:**
- Tool names: P-MEX [R15][R16]; multisource (360) feedback [R2]; critical-incident reporting.
  - CROSS-REFERENCE: See ACGME "Professionalism" for full details.

**Design and implementation details:**
- Purpose/elements/scoring/development/training: As per Professionalism record above (doctor-patient relationship, reflective skills, time management, interprofessional skills).

**Evidence of effectiveness:**

### Cross-competency synthesis
================================================================================

**Patterns across competencies:**
- Common tool types recur across most competencies: workplace-based direct-observation
  rating scales (mini-CEX, DOPS, P-MEX), multisource/360-degree feedback, OSCE/SP
  stations, portfolios, and entrustment-supervision scales tied to EPAs.
- Entrustment scales (Ottawa/O-SCORE, Chen) are increasingly the connective tissue
  between competency frameworks and daily assessment [R25][R26][R27].
- "Knowable" competencies (Medical Knowledge, EBM within PBLI/Scholar, Patient Care
  procedures) have relatively mature, psychometrically studied tools. "Intrinsic"/
  humanistic competencies (Health Advocate, Leader, reflective-practice PBLI-2,
  Systems-Based Practice) are consistently under-tooled.

**Shared strengths:**
- Strong FORMATIVE value: most tools were designed for and succeed at feedback and
  self-reflection (mini-CEX, DOPS, P-MEX, ICAR, KEECC-A).
- Structured behavioral descriptors and expert/consensus development are common and
  yield good content validity.
- Global rating scales by experts often outperform granular checklists on inter-station
  reliability and construct validity, and better capture expertise [R6][R7][R35].

**Shared limitations:**
- Reliability depends on MULTIPLE observations and MULTIPLE raters; single encounters
  are insufficient (mini-CEX, DOPS, P-MEX, entrustment scales) [R1][R3][R15][R26].
- RATER variability, leniency, halo effects, and bias are pervasive across observational
  tools; faculty-development effects on reliability are mixed [R4][R16][R27].
- Purpose confusion (formative vs summative) threatens validity [R3].
- WEAK consequential/predictive validity is the most common evidentiary gap across tools
  [R8][R12][R31]; few tools link to patient outcomes [R17].
- Time/workload and attribution problems limit feasibility and scalability, especially
  for SBP and PBLI [R11][R21][R23].
- Checklists can become reductionist/"trivial" and fail to capture reasoning or expertise
  [R7][R35].
- Much validity evidence is single-institution, single-specialty, or simulation-based;
  generalizability is frequently unproven.
- Delphi consensus found ~half of CanMEDS key competencies hard to assess consistently
  across settings/phases, with Leader and Health Advocate worst [R30].

**Implications for CBME assessment design:**
- Robust CBME needs PROGRAMMATIC assessment: triangulate many low-stakes data points
  across tools/raters/time, synthesized by competence committees, rather than relying on
  any single instrument [R27].
- Investment is most needed in the "orphan"/intrinsic competencies: Systems-Based
  Practice, Health Advocate, Leader, and reflective-practice PBLI-2 — where validated,
  feasible, generalizable tools are largely absent.
- Prefer expert global ratings + entrustment anchors over long checklists for holistic,
  developmental judgments; reserve checklists for procedural coverage [R6][R7][R35].
- Build faculty development and shared mental models into any tool deployment to reduce
  rater variance and bias.

**Considerations for LLM-augmentable assessment design:**
- LLMs can help scale narrative/qualitative evidence capture (e.g., structuring free-text
  observations, coding reflective writing/portfolios for PBLI-2, drafting formative
  feedback) — areas where human time/workload is the limiting factor.
- Machine-readable rubrics with explicit competency elements, anchored levels, and source
  citations (as in this repository) let LLMs map evidence -> guideline -> milestone level
  with traceable provenance.
- LLM outputs should be framed as FORMATIVE decision-support, not summative entrustment,
  mirroring the literature's caution that single instruments/observations cannot support
  high-stakes decisions alone [R5b][R27].
- Guard against automating known biases (rater leniency/halo, construct-irrelevant
  variance, checklist reductionism); require evidence citation and "not assessable" when
  data are absent.
